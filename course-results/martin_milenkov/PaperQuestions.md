**Question 1** - A CNV map on healthy individuals allows us to differentiate CNV patterns that are part of natural genetic variation in individuals across geography and ethnicities from patterns that are pathogenic and signal risk of disease.

**Question 2** - As defined 50Bp, though it is still a point of debate where to exactly place this threshold.

**Question 3** - Primary approaches are array-based and sequencing-based (particularly Next-Generation Sequencing, NGS). All methods are biased towards detecting deletions over duplications due to the nature of detecting CNV, however some array-based methods such as array CGH are slightly better at catching duplications, and NGS have the advantage of better catching small variants and provide higher sensitivity in general.

**Question 4** - The two criterea used are number of subjects carrying the variation, and number of studies that have at least one variant.This filtering helps prevent false positives by eliminating singletons and rare events, and are not representative for healthy population.

**Question 5** - Level 1: at least one subject carrying the variation and one study with the variant - naturally capturing every single CNV in the study; inclusive map - at least 2 subjects carrying the variation, and at least one study with the variant; stringent - at least 2 subjects and 2 studies.

**Question 6** For inclusive map - 9.5% of the human genome; for stringent map - about half at 4.8%, reflecting the expectation that the inclusive map will catch much more CNVs that may be less representative of normal healthy variation.
